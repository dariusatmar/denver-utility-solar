The Case for Utility-Scale Solar + Battery in Denver

Denver sits on one of the best solar resources in the country, pays for electricity generated mostly by natural gas and coal, and has committed to 100% renewable electricity. The cheapest new generation available — by a wide margin — is utility-scale solar photovoltaics, optionally paired with battery storage. Here is the quantitative case for the City of Denver to build it.

March 2026 · Data from NREL, EIA, Lazard, SEIA, and Xcel Energy

The Resource

Denver's Solar Irradiance Is Exceptional

Utility-scale solar economics depend on one input above all others: how much energy the sun delivers per unit area. The relevant metric is Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) for fixed-tilt systems and Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI) for trackers. Denver excels at both.

Denver's DNI is 6.12 kWh/m²/day — 5th among major US cities, behind only the desert Southwest (Las Vegas, Tucson, Albuquerque, Phoenix). This is 21% more than Miami, 7% more than Los Angeles, and 75% more than Seattle. The physics are straightforward:

  • Elevation (5,280 ft) — Denver sits above ~15% of Earth's atmosphere by mass. Less air means less Rayleigh scattering. The same sunlight carries more energy at the panel surface.
  • Aridity (~40% avg relative humidity) — Water vapor is a major absorber of solar radiation. Denver's dry air lets more through than humid cities at similar latitudes.
  • Temperature — Solar panels lose 0.3–0.5% output per °C above 25°C. Denver's cooler temperatures relative to Phoenix or Tucson mean panels operate closer to rated efficiency, partially compensating for lower raw irradiance.

Economics

Utility-Scale Solar Is the Cheapest New Electricity Available

This is no longer controversial. Lazard's 2025 LCOE+ analysis, the industry standard for generation cost comparisons, shows utility-scale solar PV at $24–96/MWh unsubsidized — cheaper than new natural gas combined cycle ($39–101/MWh), dramatically cheaper than coal ($65–152/MWh), and a fraction of gas peaker costs ($115–221/MWh).

At Denver's resource level, a well-sited utility-scale solar project achieves the lower end of that range. NREL's Annual Technology Baseline projects utility-scale PV LCOE declining to $19–28/MWh by 2035 in their moderate scenario — well below any fossil fuel source.

The cost trajectory

Solar module costs follow a 44% learning rate — costs fall 44% per doubling of cumulative global deployment. In 2024, the world deployed 446 GW of new solar capacity (equivalent to the entire global nuclear fleet), and module prices reached $0.07/W. The learning curve has held for 50 years across five orders of magnitude of deployment. There is no physical reason for it to stop.

NREL's utility-scale PV installed cost benchmark fell from $4.58/W in 2010 to under $1.00/W for large projects in 2024. Module costs are now a minority of total system cost; the remainder is labor, racking, inverters, permitting, and interconnection — all of which are also declining, though more slowly.

Compare this to natural gas, where 72% of operating cost is fuel — a commodity subject to global price volatility. Gas turbine fuel costs were $18.59/MWh in 2024 (EIA), but spiked above $40/MWh in 2022 when Henry Hub hit $9/MMBtu. Solar's fuel cost is zero. Permanently.

What Denver currently pays

Colorado's average retail electricity rate is $0.1207/kWh ($120.70/MWh). Denver's rates are slightly higher at ~$0.13–0.16/kWh depending on rate class. The city is served by Xcel Energy (Public Service Company of Colorado), whose generation mix remains ~40% natural gas and ~18% coal. Colorado's grid emits 958 lbs CO₂/MWh — ranked 16th highest nationally.

New utility-scale solar at $24–50/MWh is 2–5x cheaper than what Denver ratepayers currently pay at retail. Even accounting for transmission, distribution, and grid services costs (which make up much of the retail rate), the generation component is being overpaid relative to new solar.

Xcel's Clean Energy Plan

Xcel Energy, Denver's utility, has committed to an 80–85% carbon reduction by 2030 (from 2005 levels) and 100% carbon-free by 2050. In January 2024, the Colorado PUC approved Phase II of Xcel's Clean Energy Plan: 6,100 MW of new generation — the largest clean energy portfolio in state history, representing $12–13 billion in investment.

Xcel plans to retire all coal by 2030. The replacement portfolio is overwhelmingly solar, wind, and battery storage. This is not altruism — it's economics. When Xcel solicited competitive bids in 2022, they received over 1,000 project proposals. Solar+storage consistently won on price.

But Xcel alone isn't enough

Xcel's plan is significant but faces execution risk: permitting delays, transmission bottlenecks, and interconnection queues. Colorado's grid interconnection queue has grown dramatically. Meanwhile, Denver's electricity demand is growing — driven by electrification of buildings and transport, population growth, and data center demand along the Front Range.

The city has leverage to accelerate this transition beyond what a regulated utility will deliver on its own timeline. Municipal solar procurement, community solar at scale, direct PPAs for city operations, and brownfield development all represent paths that don't require waiting for Xcel's interconnection queue.

Storage

Battery Storage Makes Solar Dispatchable

The standard objection to utility-scale solar — "the sun doesn't always shine" — has a straightforward engineering answer: batteries. The question is whether the economics work. They do.

Battery cell costs have fallen from $700+/kWh in 2013 to $45/kWh in 2025 (LFP, cell level), driven by a 23% learning rate. Pack-level costs for utility-scale systems (including BMS, enclosure, thermal management) are approximately $150–200/kWh. These costs are still falling.

Handmer's utilization framework

Casey Handmer's analysis of solar+battery systems provides a useful framework for sizing storage. Using real NREL 5-minute interval solar data, he found that the optimal battery-to-solar ratio depends on the capital cost of the load being served:

  • Low-capex loads (<$1,000/kW): Intermittent operation. No batteries needed. 21–33% utilization. This is the regime for industrial processes like synthetic fuel production.
  • High-capex loads (>$1,000/kW): 4–15 kWh of battery per kW of load, achieving 90–98% utilization with daily charge/discharge cycling.
  • Beyond 15 kWh/kW of battery: Diminishing returns. Additional solar array overbuild is more cost-effective than rarely-used battery capacity.

Grid applications fall squarely in the battery-backed regime. A 4-hour battery (the current industry standard for utility-scale) paired with solar achieves an LCOE of $46–102/MWh (Lazard) — still competitive with new gas generation and dramatically cheaper than peakers.

Grid services beyond energy shifting

Utility-scale batteries provide value beyond time-shifting solar generation:

  • Frequency regulation — batteries respond in milliseconds, far faster than gas turbines
  • Peak shaving — reduces need for expensive peaker plants that run only a few hundred hours per year
  • Transmission deferral — strategically sited storage can delay or eliminate costly grid upgrades
  • Resilience — distributed battery sites provide backup during grid outages, including wildfire and winter storm events increasingly common on the Front Range

Colorado's Microgrids for Community Resilience Program provides grant funding for projects that improve community resilience through distributed energy resources including battery storage — a direct funding pathway for municipal battery projects.

Land

Where to Put It: Land Requirements and Site Options

A common concern with utility-scale solar: does Denver have the space? The math is more favorable than most people expect.

How much land does Denver need?

Denver's total electricity consumption is approximately 9.5 TWh/year (city proper, ~730,000 people). A utility-scale solar plant with single-axis tracking in Denver's resource class produces about 450–500 MWh/MW/year (30–34% capacity factor × 8,760 hours, adjusted for losses).

To generate 100% of Denver's electricity from solar:

This is roughly 2–3% of Adams County, the largely agricultural county directly north and east of Denver. Or about 0.5% of the eastern Colorado plains within 100 miles of Denver. The land requirement is real but modest — equivalent to a mid-size ranch.

For context: Colorado has 66.5 million acres of land. 24,000 acres is 0.036% of the state.

Site options

Denver doesn't need to develop pristine land. Several categories of existing land are well-suited for utility-scale solar:

  • Eastern plains agricultural land — Weld, Adams, and Arapahoe counties have abundant flat, low-value agricultural land at $1,000–$3,000/acre. Solar leases ($800–1,200/acre/year) can significantly exceed farming income, making this attractive to landowners.
  • DIA buffer zone — Denver International Airport controls ~34,000 acres (53 sq miles), much of it buffer land. Airport-adjacent solar is increasingly common nationally (Indianapolis, Denver, Tucson have all explored it). DIA's flat terrain and existing grid interconnection make it a natural candidate.
  • Brownfields and capped landfills — EPA's RE-Powering program identifies contaminated sites suitable for solar. Denver has several closed landfills and former industrial sites that cannot be developed for other purposes.
  • Canal and reservoir covers — Floating solar on water reservoirs reduces evaporation (significant in Colorado's water-scarce climate) and improves panel efficiency through cooling. Denver Water manages multiple reservoirs.
  • Xcel-owned sites — As coal plants retire by 2030, their sites — already equipped with grid interconnection and transmission capacity — become prime locations for solar+battery replacement.

The DIA opportunity

DIA's 34,000-acre footprint is larger than the entire land requirement for 100% of Denver's electricity. Even using 10% of the buffer land (3,400 acres) for solar would yield ~500–700 MW of generation — enough for 5–7% of Denver's total consumption. The airport already has high-voltage grid interconnection and is city-owned land. No land acquisition needed.

Policy

Denver's Policy Position and the Path Forward

Existing commitments

Denver has already committed substantial resources to climate action:

  • Climate Protection Fund — $40+ million/year dedicated to climate action, funded by a voter-approved sales tax increase
  • 100% renewable electricity goal — Denver has committed to 100% renewable electricity for city operations and is working toward community-wide targets
  • Office of Climate Action, Sustainability and Resiliency — Dedicated city agency with staff and budget

These commitments create both the mandate and the institutional capacity for large-scale solar procurement.

Colorado's regulatory framework

Colorado's state-level policy is increasingly favorable for utility-scale solar:

  • SB19-236 — Requires investor-owned utilities (including Xcel) to file Clean Energy Plans with the PUC and evaluate energy storage in all resource planning
  • SB24-218 — Distributed Generation Grant and Apprenticeship program, investing in renewable energy workforce
  • SB24-207 — Modernizing the distribution system for solar and storage interconnection
  • No sales tax on solar equipment — Reduces project costs by ~3%
  • Property tax exemption for solar — Avoids tax burden on solar asset value

Procurement options

Denver has several paths to utility-scale solar, each with different risk profiles and timelines:

Approach Pros Cons
Municipal PPA No upfront capital. Fixed long-term rates. Developer handles construction and O&M. City doesn't own the asset. Less control over siting and timing.
City-owned generation Full ownership. Long-term rate control. Revenue from excess generation. Upfront capital ($3–5B for full supply). Requires municipal bonding or financing.
Community solar at scale Leverages existing state framework. Distributed benefit. No transmission needed. Smaller scale per project. More complex administration.
Negotiate with Xcel Works within existing utility structure. Xcel has Clean Energy Plan in progress. City dependent on Xcel's timeline and priorities. Less direct control.
Municipal utility Full control over generation, rates, and planning. Proven model (Austin, Sacramento, LA). Most complex path. Requires buying out Xcel's distribution assets. Legal and political battle.

The most pragmatic near-term approach is likely a combination: municipal PPAs for city operations (covering municipal buildings, street lighting, water treatment, transit) while negotiating an accelerated clean energy schedule with Xcel for the broader rate base. Longer term, a municipal utility remains on the table — Boulder pursued this path for years before reaching a settlement with Xcel in 2020.

What other cities have done

Denver would not be the first city to pursue this at scale:

  • Austin Energy (TX) — Municipal utility serving 500,000+ customers. Procured 1,500+ MW of solar via PPAs at $20–25/MWh. On track for 65% renewable by 2027.
  • Sacramento (SMUD) — Municipal utility. Signed contracts for 1,000+ MW of solar+storage. Targets 100% carbon-free by 2030.
  • Los Angeles (LADWP) — Largest municipal utility in the US. Building the 400 MW Eland Solar+Storage project, contracted at $20/MWh for solar — one of the cheapest PPAs in US history.
  • Pueblo, CO — Formed its own municipal utility in 2020, purchasing distribution assets from Black Hills Energy. Pursuing aggressive solar procurement.
Opportunity

The Data Center Opportunity

AI and cloud computing are driving unprecedented electricity demand growth along the Front Range. Data centers require reliable, high-capacity power — and they're increasingly willing to co-locate with or directly procure renewable generation.

Casey Handmer's analysis of data center power requirements shows that solar+battery can achieve 98–99.9% uptime for even high-capex loads ($50,000/kW for AI compute), at a power supply cost of ~$4/W — under 10% of total data center capital cost. The economics are compelling: a 1 GW solar-powered data center requires ~20,000 acres and ~$60 billion in total development cost, with the solar+battery portion under $4 billion.

Denver's combination of excellent solar resource, available land east of the metro, fiber connectivity, and cool climate (reducing cooling costs) makes it an attractive candidate for solar-powered data center development. Cities that can offer clean, cheap, abundant power will win the data center competition. Utility-scale solar is how Denver gets there.

Summary

The Numbers Add Up

Denver has:

  • A top-5 solar resource among major US cities, with 29–34% utility-scale capacity factors
  • A grid still 40% natural gas and 18% coal, emitting 958 lbs CO₂/MWh
  • $40+ million/year in climate action funding
  • Available land (eastern plains, DIA buffer, brownfields, retired coal sites) sufficient for 100% solar generation
  • A state regulatory framework that is actively encouraging solar+storage deployment
  • An investor-owned utility (Xcel) already planning 6,100 MW of new clean generation

Utility-scale solar at $24–50/MWh is the cheapest new electricity source available. Paired with 4-hour batteries at $46–102/MWh, it's dispatchable and competitive with new gas. The costs are falling by ~15% per year. Every year Denver waits, it pays more for gas-generated electricity while solar gets cheaper.

The engineering is proven. The economics are favorable. The land exists. The policy framework supports it. The remaining question is execution speed.
